Weak Voice of the Younger Generation in Japanese Politics

By Shotaro Miyasaka | July 8, 2023 | Data sources are available in Github

Photo by Shotaro Miyasaka

In Japan, citizens can run for election to the House of Representatives at the age of 25. However, in the most recent general election in 2021, only one 29-year-old man was elected under the age of 30.

Now, there is no member of the House in his or her 20s, and only about 5% of all members are 30s. This is the lowest level among the seven G7 countries, the U.S., U.K., France, Germany, Japan, Italy, and Canada's lower house.

Only 23 of 465 seats

In the 2021 general election, less than 5 percent of the winners were under the age of 40.

Source:Japan's House of Representatives

According to data from the Inter-Parliamentary Union, more than a quarter of the members of the Bundestag in Germany and the National Assembly in France are under 40, and 20% of the members of the House of Commons in the UK are under 40. In the U.S. House of Representatives, about 10% of the members are in their 20s or 30s, about double the proportion in Japan.

Japan has one of the highest aging populations in the world. Even so, the number of young members of the House of Representatives is still small compared to the population. The United Nations estimates that in 2021, 15% of Japan's population will be between the ages of 25 and 39. The seats they won in that year's general election were one-third of that percentage.

What about the world as a whole? Using data from the Inter-Parliamentary Union, we analyzed differences by region and electoral system.

In terms of electoral systems, proportional representation systems tend to have more young senators than majority representation systems. U.S. senators and representatives elected under the majority representation system are less likely to be young. Japan is classified as a mixed system, with more than 60% of the population elected in primary elections.

There are also differences by region. In many parts of Asia, including Japan, less than 20% of the population is under the age of 40. In contrast, in Europe, there is a majority of parliaments with more than 20% of the population, and in particular, there is not a single example of a House of Representatives with less than 10%.

In the G7, the age of suffrage for House of Representatives elections in five countries except the U.S. and Japan is 18. In Japan and the U.S., the age of suffrage is 25, although it is possible to vote from the age of 18.

In Japan, the percentage of female legislators is as low as about 10%. To change the situation, the law obliges political parties to make an effort to strike a balance. The fact that the voices of young people are not easily reflected is another lack of diversity.
