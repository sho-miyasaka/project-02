Weak Voice of the Younger Generation in Japanese Politics

By Shotaro Miyasaka | July 8, 2023 | Data sources are available in Github

Photo by Shotaro Miyasaka

In Japan, citizens can run for election to the House of Representatives at the age of 25. However, in the most recent general election in 2021, only one 29-year-old man was elected under the age of 30.

Now, there is no member of the House in his or her 20s, and only about 5% of all members are 30s. This is the lowest level among the seven G7 countries's lower house.

Only 23 of 465 seats

In the 2021 general election, less than 5 percent of the winners were under the age of 40.

Source:Japan's House of Representatives

According to the Inter-Parliamentary Union, more than a quarter of the members of the German Bundestag and the French National Assembly are under 40, and the House of Commons in the UK has over 20% of its members under the age of 40. The U.S. House of Representatives has a relatively low percentage of members in their 20s and 30s, but the Japanese lower house has a much lower percentage.

Japan has one of the most aging populations in the world. Even so, the number of young members of the House of Representatives is still small compared to the population. The United Nations estimates that by 2021, 15% of Japan's population will be between the ages of 25 and 39. The seats they won in this year's general election were one-third of that percentage.

What about the world overall? Using data from the Inter-Parliamentary Union, we analyzed differences by region and electoral system.

In terms of electoral systems, proportional representation systems tend to have more young members than plurality representation systems. U.S. senators and representatives elected under the plurality system are less likely to be young. Japan's electoral system is classified as a mixed system, with more than 60% of the population elected in plurality elections.

There are also regional differences. In most Asian countries, including Japan, less than 20% of the population is under the age of 40. In Europe, on the other hand, there are a majority of parliaments with more than 20% of the population, and in particular, there is not a single example of a House of Representatives with less than 10%.

In the G7, the eligibility age for lower house elections is 18 in five countries except the U.S. and Japan. In Japan and the U.S., the eligibility age is 25, although it is possible to vote from the age of 18. This factor may have also influenced the difference.

In Japan, the percentage of female legislators is as low as about 10%. To change the situation, the law obliges political parties to make an effort to strike a balance. The fact that the voices of young people are not easily reflected is another lack of diversity.
